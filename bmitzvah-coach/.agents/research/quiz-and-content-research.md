# Quiz and Content Research

Research backing the B'Mitzvah 2.0 interest quiz and the six journey content banks. Scope: how validated instruments assess interests and strengths in 10 to 13 year olds, how our six templates map onto those frameworks, and a proposed quiz plus per-template activity banks built from real programs.

Tone rules applied throughout: modern app, smart older sibling, no school or institutional vibes, Jewish concepts glossed in plain words, and no em dashes anywhere.

---

## Question design principles

Do's and don'ts for quizzing 10 to 13 year olds, each tied to a source.

- Keep it to 8 to 12 questions and under 10 minutes total. Attention drops fast at this age and a tired kid just clicks the last option they read. https://conjointly.com/blog/best-practice-guidelines-for-conducting-surveys-with-children/
- Cap answer options at 4 to 6. Kids age 11 and up can reliably hold about five choices in mind at once; longer lists cause overload and random picking. https://business.yougov.com/content/46884-ten-tips-survey-children
- Write at a grade 5 reading level and verify it (Flesch-Kincaid). Short sentences, common words, zero jargon. https://heysurvey.io/examples/survey-questions-for-kids
- Use concrete scenarios, not abstract self-labels. Ask "what would you do on a free Saturday" instead of "how creative are you." Kids reason from real experiences, not from rating traits. https://conjointly.com/blog/best-practice-guidelines-for-conducting-surveys-with-children/
- Prefer forced choice and word-based options over number scales. Children answer 1 to 5 number scales with very low consistency (under 35 percent), while word choices are far more reliable. https://conjointly.com/blog/best-practice-guidelines-for-conducting-surveys-with-children/
- Never ask two things in one question. Kids answer only the last clause they read, so "do you like art and building things" is really two questions. https://conjointly.com/blog/best-practice-guidelines-for-conducting-surveys-with-children/
- Avoid "not" and negatives. Kids frequently misread negatively phrased questions. Phrase every option as a positive thing you would choose. https://conjointly.com/blog/best-practice-guidelines-for-conducting-surveys-with-children/
- Keep options short and about the same length so no answer looks like the "right" one just because it has more words. https://heysurvey.io/examples/survey-questions-for-kids
- Balance the options with neutral framing ("some kids like this, other kids like that") so no single answer reads as the cool or correct pick. https://conjointly.com/blog/best-practice-guidelines-for-conducting-surveys-with-children/
- Add an emoji or picture to every option. For this age, visuals act as external memory and cut the reading load. https://conjointly.com/blog/best-practice-guidelines-for-conducting-surveys-with-children/
- Open with an easy, fun, low-stakes question to build momentum, like "pick 3 words that feel like you," before any scenario. https://business.yougov.com/content/46884-ten-tips-survey-children
- Borrow structure from validated youth instruments instead of inventing scales. Gallup's CliftonStrengths / StrengthsExplorer for ages 10 to 14 uses 76 paired-comparison items in about 15 minutes, and the VIA Youth Survey measures 24 strengths for ages 10 to 17. Paired or forced choice over interest and strength domains is the proven pattern. https://support.gallup.com/hc/en-us/articles/52542461518483-What-is-StrengthsExplorer and https://www.viacharacter.org/reports/youth

## Framework mapping

Two youth-validated frameworks anchor the templates:

- RIASEC (Holland Codes): six interest types, Realistic, Investigative, Artistic, Social, Enterprising, Conventional. The O*NET Interest Profiler is the public version, with pictorial adaptations built for younger and lower-reading students. https://www.onetcenter.org/reports/IP_Manual.html and https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holland_Codes
- VIA Youth Character Strengths: 24 strengths grouped as Wisdom, Courage, Humanity, Justice, Temperance, Transcendence, validated for ages 10 to 17. https://www.viacharacter.org/reports/youth

| Template | Framework anchors | Justification with sources |
|---|---|---|
| into-the-wild | RIASEC Realistic + Investigative; VIA zest, bravery, appreciation of beauty and nature; interest domain: outdoors, animals, environment | Realistic types are hands-on and drawn to tools, animals, and the outdoors, and Investigative curiosity fits kids who want to understand nature, which is exactly the outdoor-immersion and wilderness-skill model real programs use (fire skills, trail reading, nature connection). https://jobcannon.io/blog/riasec-career-types-deep-dive and https://wildernesstorah.org/programs/youth-and-family-programs/bnaiture/ |
| make-something-real | RIASEC Artistic; VIA creativity, appreciation of beauty; interest domain: making, performing, crafting | Artistic types prefer unstructured, expressive work and value imagination over rules, which is the maker, performer, and designer profile. Character-strength creativity is the through-line. https://jobcannon.io/blog/riasec-career-types-deep-dive and https://www.viacharacter.org/reports/youth |
| make-a-difference | RIASEC Social + Enterprising; VIA kindness, fairness, teamwork, leadership; interest domain: service, social impact | Social types are helpers who thrive on care and teamwork, and Enterprising adds the organizing and leadership needed to run a real project. VIA kindness and fairness are the justice-and-humanity strengths, matching Jewish service work (Tikkun Olam, which means repairing the world). https://talentinsights.com/blog/holland-code-personality-types/ and https://www.viacharacter.org/reports/youth and https://werepair.org/get-involved/volunteer/ |
| mind-and-meaning | RIASEC Investigative; VIA love of learning, curiosity, judgment, perspective; interest domain: ideas, research, study | Investigative types are curious analyzers who enjoy research, questions, and complex problems, and VIA love of learning plus curiosity name the same drive. This is the independent-study and deep-dive path where kids become the expert on a chosen question. https://jobcannon.io/blog/riasec-career-types-deep-dive and https://whyy.org/articles/can-a-modern-spin-on-a-bar-bat-mitzvah-tradition-keep-teens-in-the-jewish-community/ |
| roots-and-rituals | VIA spirituality, gratitude, love; CliftonStrengths Relating; interest domain: family memory, heritage, tradition as choice | This template rides on connection and meaning, not religiosity. VIA love and gratitude plus Gallup's Relating theme (deep one-to-one bonds) fit kids drawn to family stories, recipes, and heritage. Interfaith legacy projects (recording grandparents, family trees, the story behind an object) are the concrete form. https://www.viacharacter.org/reports/youth and https://strengthszone.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/01/Clifton-Youth-Strengths-Explorer2016.pdf and https://18doors.org/interfaith-familys-bar-mitzvah/ |
| my-own-path | High openness / eclectic profiles; VIA creativity + curiosity + independent streak; CliftonStrengths Discoverer + Confidence; interest domain: mix-everything, do-it-your-way | Most people show a blend of two or three Holland types rather than one, so a kid with no single spike or a wide spread belongs here. Gallup's Discoverer (asks why, loves figuring out) and Confidence themes name the independent, self-directing profile, matching the fully custom, choose-your-own journey. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holland_Codes and https://strengthszone.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/01/Clifton-Youth-Strengths-Explorer2016.pdf |

Template keys used in weights below: IW = into-the-wild, MSR = make-something-real, MAD = make-a-difference, MM = mind-and-meaning, RR = roots-and-rituals, MOP = my-own-path.

## Proposed quiz (10 questions)

Warm, grade 5, second person, no religious framing, no template names shown to the kid. Weights are internal only. Points range 1 to 3 and land on 1 to 3 templates per option.

### Q1. Pick exactly 3 words that feel like you.

Twelve options, choose three. Each word carries weight.

| Word | Emoji | Weights | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| Adventurous | 🧭 | IW +2 | Outdoor, Realistic pull toward exploring and doing. |
| Wild | 🌲 | IW +2 | Nature and the outdoors as a home base. |
| Creative | 🎨 | MSR +2 | Core Artistic and VIA creativity marker. |
| Funny | 🎭 | MSR +1, MOP +1 | Performing energy, and a playful streak that resists one lane. |
| Caring | 💛 | MAD +2 | VIA kindness, the Social helper drive. |
| Organized | 📋 | MAD +1, MM +1 | Enterprising organizing for projects, plus a methodical mind. |
| Curious | 🔍 | MM +2 | Investigative and VIA curiosity, wants to know how things work. |
| Thoughtful | 🧠 | MM +2 | Reflective, idea-driven, VIA love of learning and perspective. |
| Cozy | 🕯️ | RR +2 | Warmth of home, candles, meals, family togetherness. |
| Loyal | 🤝 | RR +2 | Deep bonds and belonging, the Relating and love strength. |
| Dreamer | ☁️ | MOP +2 | Open, eclectic, imagines its own thing. |
| Bold | 🚀 | MOP +1, MAD +1 | Self-directed and willing to lead a cause. |

Every template is reachable in Q1: IW (Adventurous, Wild), MSR (Creative, Funny), MAD (Caring, Organized, Bold), MM (Curious, Thoughtful, Organized), RR (Cozy, Loyal), MOP (Dreamer, Bold, Funny).

## Template content bank

Every activity lists: title, description, kind (do / create / learn / give), and where it is inspired by. Real program names are inspiration only, nothing is copied.

### into-the-wild

Nature, outdoors, and the environment. For the kid who feels most themselves under open sky.

Activities:
1. Trail You Restore. Adopt a nearby trail and make it your project: clear litter, log the plants and animals you spot, and leave it better than you found it. kind: do. Inspired by Wilderness Torah nature immersion and park-cleanup mitzvah projects. https://wildernesstorah.org/programs/youth-and-family-programs/bnaiture/
2. Plant A Pollinator Patch. Grow a small butterfly and bee garden with native flowers and track who shows up. kind: do. Inspired by butterfly-garden and seed-bomb project ideas. https://bestprojectideas.com/mitzvah-project-ideas/
3. One Night Under Stars. Plan a supervised overnight campout and learn to build a safe fire and read a trail map. kind: learn. Inspired by the Wilderness Torah fire-tending solo. https://wildernesstorah.org/programs/youth-and-family-programs/bnaiture/faq/
4. Grow And Give Garden. Start a vegetable patch and donate the harvest to a food bank. kind: give. Inspired by summer-garden-to-food-bank mitzvah projects. https://www.onlinejewishlearning.com/how-to-choose-the-perfect-bar-bat-mitzvah-project-ideas/
5. Shelter Makeover Day. Volunteer at an animal rescue: make pet toys, walk dogs, and help paint an adoption room. kind: give. Inspired by ASPCA and "Bark Mitzvah" animal-shelter projects. https://www.mitzvah-maker.com/animals-and-environment
6. Map Your Wild Places. Build a photo map of local wild spots and write a short field guide to share. kind: create. Inspired by naturalist field journaling.
7. Cleanup Crew You Lead. Organize a beach, river, or park cleanup with friends and weigh what you haul out. kind: do. Inspired by Reform Movement mitzvah-day cleanup projects. https://rac.org/project-ideas-mitzvah-days
8. Learn One Outdoor Skill. Pick a real skill (knots, navigation, first aid, foraging basics) and get good enough to teach it. kind: learn. Inspired by Adventure Rabbi outdoor-skills classes. https://www.adventurerabbi.org/

Celebration ideas:
- An outdoor ceremony at a favorite park, lake, or trailhead that ends with everyone planting something together.
- A campfire or sunrise gathering where you share what a year outdoors taught you.

Getting started this week:
- Pick one outdoor spot you can reach easily and go visit it with a notebook.
- Text two friends who would actually show up for a hike or a cleanup and set a date.
- Find your closest park district or animal shelter online and email to ask what they need.

### make-something-real

Creative, performance, craft, and maker. For the kid who needs to build, draw, film, or perform to feel done.

Activities:
1. Your Own Art Show. Make a series of pieces, host a show, sell prints, and give the money to a cause. kind: create. Inspired by student art-show mitzvah projects. https://www.mitzvahmarket.com/projects/bar-bat-mitzvah-projects-from-a-to-z/
2. Write And Record A Song. Write an original song about something you believe in and record it for real. kind: create. Inspired by "write a song for charity" project ideas. https://mitzvahrabbi.com/mitzvah-project-ideas-bar-mitzvah-and-bat-mitzvah-project-suggestions/
3. Short Film You Direct. Script, shoot, and edit a short film or mini documentary about your world. kind: create. Inspired by film and visual-media b'nai mitzvah projects.
4. Instruments For Kids. Collect used instruments and give them to a school music program that needs gear. kind: give. Inspired by instrument-collection projects for underfunded music programs. https://www.mitzvahmarket.com/projects/bar-bat-mitzvah-projects-from-a-to-z/
5. Design The Whole Look. Design your own invitations, logo, and space so the day looks exactly like you. kind: create. Inspired by the you-are-the-designer product principle.
6. Perform Your Story. Write and perform a monologue, spoken word piece, or short set about becoming who you are. kind: create. Inspired by the "perform a monologue instead of a speech" alternative. https://www.myjewishlearning.com/article/parents-barbat-mitzvah-preparation/
7. Build A Real Thing. Use wood, code, or fabric to build something useful and give it away, like park benches, an app, or blankets. kind: create. Inspired by maker mitzvah projects.
8. Teach A Craft Workshop. Run a hands-on craft station and teach younger kids your skill. kind: do. Inspired by DIY craft-station b'nai mitzvah ideas. https://www.mitzvahmarket.com/projects/bar-bat-mitzvah-projects-from-a-to-z/

Celebration ideas:
- Turn the celebration into a gallery or premiere night where your work is the main event.
- A live set you plan and headline: a band, a DJ booth, or a spoken-word stage.

Getting started this week:
- Pick the one medium you already love and set a tiny first deadline, like one piece by Friday.
- Follow three makers whose work you love and copy one technique to learn it.
- Ask a local cafe, library, or studio if you can show or perform your work there.

### make-a-difference

Service and social impact. In Jewish words this is Tikkun Olam, which means repairing the world. For the kid who wants to leave things better than they found them.

Activities:
1. Fill A Community Fridge. Organize food drives and keep a local free fridge or pantry stocked. kind: give. Inspired by Repair the World community-fridge fills. https://werepair.org/get-involved/volunteer/
2. Bake To Fund A Cause. Run a bake sale or bake-for-change to raise money and attention for something you care about. kind: give. Inspired by Challah for Hunger. https://werepair.org/get-involved/volunteer/
3. Care Packages At Scale. Assemble hygiene and comfort kits for shelters or hospitals: toiletries, hats, socks, small games. kind: give. Inspired by shelter hospitality-bag projects. https://tbsmb.org/mitzvah-day-project-descriptions/
4. Cheer Squad For Hospitals. Train to visit sick kids or seniors with games, jokes, and balloon animals. kind: do. Inspired by the Areyvut Mitzvah Clowns program. https://areyvut.org/2016/01/areyvut-from-a-z/
5. Run A Real Fundraiser. Plan a walk, tournament, or event from start to finish and pick where the money goes. kind: do. Inspired by Areyvut bowl-a-thons and fundraising walks. https://areyvut.org/2016/01/areyvut-from-a-z/
6. Glean And Rescue Food. Join a food-rescue crew that harvests extra produce for people who need it. kind: give. Inspired by Leket and Project Leket gleaning. https://areyvut.org/2016/01/areyvut-from-a-z/
7. Pen Pal With A Purpose. Become a pen pal or tech buddy for homebound seniors. kind: do. Inspired by Areyvut intergenerational programs. https://areyvut.org/2016/01/areyvut-from-a-z/
8. Map A Problem You'd Fix. Pick one issue in your town, research it, and pitch a small fix to a local group. kind: learn. Inspired by the Repair the World service-learning model. https://werepair.org/get-involved/volunteer/

Celebration ideas:
- A give-back party: ask for donations to your cause instead of gifts and show guests the impact.
- Fold a group service project into the day so everyone leaves having helped.

Getting started this week:
- Name the one thing about the world that makes you angry or sad, and start there.
- Find one local org already doing that work and ask how a 12 year old can plug in.
- Set a small real goal this week, like collecting 50 items or raising your first 50 dollars.

### mind-and-meaning

Learning, ideas, and independent study. For the kid who wants to become the expert and tell everyone what they found.

Activities:
1. Deep Dive On One Question. Pick a big question you actually wonder about and spend weeks becoming the expert. kind: learn. Inspired by independent-study b'nai mitzvah projects on topics like Jewish pirates and "does God exist." https://whyy.org/articles/can-a-modern-spin-on-a-bar-bat-mitzvah-tradition-keep-teens-in-the-jewish-community/
2. Teach A Mini Lecture. Turn what you learned into a short, fun, TED-style talk. kind: learn. Inspired by the dissertation-style presentation model. https://whyy.org/articles/can-a-modern-spin-on-a-bar-bat-mitzvah-tradition-keep-teens-in-the-jewish-community/
3. Start A Podcast Or Zine. Interview people and publish what you find out about your topic. kind: create. Inspired by independent-learning-project framing.
4. Retell An Old Story New. Take an old family or culture story and rewrite it with your own meaning. kind: create. Inspired by writing an original midrash, which just means making your own new meaning from an old story. https://www.myjewishlearning.com/article/parents-barbat-mitzvah-preparation/
5. Build A Working Model. Turn an idea into a real experiment, model, or piece of code that proves your point. kind: create. Inspired by the investigative and maker crossover.
6. Read Across A Whole Theme. Read five books or watch five talks on one theme and map how they connect. kind: learn. Inspired by the "read books and attend lectures" study model. https://whyy.org/articles/can-a-modern-spin-on-a-bar-bat-mitzvah-tradition-keep-teens-in-the-jewish-community/
7. Interview The Experts. Find real people who know your topic and interview them for what books leave out. kind: learn. Inspired by primary-research approaches.
8. Write What You Believe. Write a short essay or manifesto on one value you have thought hard about. kind: create. Inspired by "answer a big question" independent projects.

Celebration ideas:
- A salon evening where you present your findings and take real questions from guests.
- A printed booklet or website of your research handed to everyone who comes.

Getting started this week:
- Write down the one question you would stay up late reading about and make it your project.
- Get a library card or one strong source this week and start taking notes.
- Book one 15 minute interview with someone who knows more than you.

### roots-and-rituals

Tradition-curious, ritual as a choice, and family memory. Built for interfaith families, this is about connection and heritage, never about being religious enough. A ritual here just means a small meaningful thing you do on purpose.

Activities:
1. Record A Grandparent. Interview an older relative on video about their life and save it for the whole family. kind: learn. Inspired by grandparent memory-recording and family legacy projects. https://jewishjournal.com/news/171785/from-grandparent-to-child-recording-memories/
2. Cook The Family Cookbook. Collect recipes passed down in your family and cook a full meal from them. kind: create. Inspired by cooking family recipes and learning the foods relatives loved. https://www.myjewishlearning.com/article/special-planning-issues-for-interfaith-families/
3. Build A Family Tree. Map your family across countries and faiths and tell the story of how you got here. kind: learn. Inspired by interfaith family-legacy tree projects. https://18doors.org/interfaith-familys-bar-mitzvah/
4. The Story Behind An Object. Find one meaningful family object and record the story attached to it. kind: learn. Inspired by asking relatives about heirlooms and the stories attached. https://18doors.org/files/pdf/Bar_BatMitzvahIdeasandPrimerforInterfaithFamilies.pdf
5. Design Your Own Ritual. Invent a small ceremony that feels like you, borrowing bits you love: candles, a toast, a walk, a song. kind: create. Inspired by interfaith custom-ceremony and ritual-as-choice ideas. https://18doors.org/interfaith-familys-bar-mitzvah/
6. Light And Share Night. Host a monthly candle-lit dinner where everyone shares a high and a low from the week. kind: do. Inspired by the weekly Shabbat family dinner, which is just a set night to slow down and eat together with candles.
7. Make A Memory Book. Build a scrapbook or photo book of your family's people, places, and stories. kind: create. Inspired by legacy projects using family documents, photos, and memoirs. https://18doors.org/interfaith-familys-bar-mitzvah/
8. Learn Where You Come From. Pick one side of your family and learn its traditions, language, or holidays. kind: learn. Inspired by interfaith heritage-honoring projects. https://18doors.org/interfaith-familys-bar-mitzvah/

Celebration ideas:
- A multi-generation dinner where each generation shares a story or a blessing in their own words.
- A display of family photos and objects with the story you collected placed next to each one.

Getting started this week:
- Call or sit with one older relative this week and ask them a single question about their childhood.
- Pick the family recipe you love most and find out who it came from.
- Start a notes file for every family story you hear so none of them get lost.

### my-own-path

Fully custom, eclectic, and independent. For the kid whose answer is "why not all of it, my way."

Activities:
1. Remix Two Journeys. Take pieces from two different journeys and blend them into one that is only yours. kind: create. Inspired by personalizing the milestone to your own interests. https://www.myjewishlearning.com/article/parents-barbat-mitzvah-preparation/
2. The 30 Day Challenge. Design a personal challenge, a skill, a streak, or a quest, and document all 30 days. kind: do. Inspired by the gamified, kid-as-designer product model.
3. Build Your Own Rulebook. Write your own list of what this milestone means and what you will do to earn it. kind: create. Inspired by building a stronger, more independent identity. https://www.myjewishlearning.com/article/parents-barbat-mitzvah-preparation/
4. Sampler Season. Try one activity from every other journey and keep the ones that click. kind: do. Inspired by Areyvut Tikkun Olam Tour "many opportunities in one day" days. https://areyvut.org/2016/01/areyvut-from-a-z/
5. Passion Into A Project. Take the thing you are already obsessed with and turn it into your whole journey. kind: create. Inspired by passion-based b'nai mitzvah projects. https://whyy.org/articles/can-a-modern-spin-on-a-bar-bat-mitzvah-tradition-keep-teens-in-the-jewish-community/
6. Collaborate With A Mentor. Find one adult who does something you admire and learn by doing it alongside them. kind: learn. Inspired by mentor and apprentice models like Wilderness Torah youth mentors. https://wildernesstorah.org/programs/youth-and-family-programs/bnaiture/
7. Make A Time Capsule. Capture who you are right now in a box or a file to open at 18. kind: create. Inspired by identity and milestone framing.
8. Document The Whole Thing. Vlog, blog, or journal your journey so the process itself is the project. kind: create. Inspired by process-as-project maker culture.

Celebration ideas:
- A celebration you fully art-direct, mixing whatever you love: outdoor, music, food, and all.
- An unveiling where you reveal the custom thing you built and explain why you did it your way.

Getting started this week:
- List five things you love that do not usually go together and look for the overlap.
- Steal one idea from each of the other journeys and mix them into a shortlist.
- Give your journey a name only you would pick and write it somewhere you will see it.
