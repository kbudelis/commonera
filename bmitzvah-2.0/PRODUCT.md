# Product

## Register

product

## Platform

web

## Users

Primary: the child, 10 to 13 (persona: Leo, 12, multicultural household). Not motivated by Hebrew or Torah study. Wants agency, to design something that reflects who he is today. Will disengage the moment it feels like adults took over. Drawn to creative projects, outdoor challenges, or social impact.

Secondary: the parent (persona: Maya, interfaith household, part of the "70%" of unaffiliated or culturally Jewish families). Finds traditional models rigid and archaic, wants to mark the milestone meaningfully, needs confidence more than control. Her quiet worries: are we doing it right, is this Jewish enough, will Leo actually care.

The child gets the main creative surface (quiz, journey, dashboard). The parent gets a calm read-mostly dashboard plus account and kid registration.

## Product Purpose

A child-led digital platform that helps unaffiliated Jewish families design and complete a personalized B'Mitzvah journey on their terms. A personality quiz puts the child in the driver's seat, six journey templates solve the blank-page problem, a personal dashboard makes progress visible and motivating, and a simulated provider directory shows how real-world fulfilment works. Success: a 12-year-old gets from landing to a named journey on a dashboard in under 5 minutes and feels proud enough to show someone.

## Positioning

Your B'Mitzvah. Your way. The only milestone platform where the kid is the designer, not the student.

## Brand Personality

Warm, empowering, personal. Openly modern, structured without being institutional, Jewish without being religious. Feels like Spotify (personal identity, your soundtrack) crossed with Duolingo (friendly gamified progress) and Canva (you are the designer). Tone of a smart older sibling, never a curriculum. Celebration is the destination: every screen builds toward something joyful and earned.

## Anti-references

Hebrew school platforms, synagogue signup pages, and Jewish institutional websites. Generic coming-of-age or edtech apps. Blue-and-white flag aesthetics, Stars of David or ancient motifs without fresh context. School checklists. Anything a 13-year-old would find embarrassingly formal or embarrassingly babyish. If a reviewer would use the words "religious", "school", or "institutional", it has failed.

## Design Principles

1. The kid is the designer. Every choice on a child surface belongs to the child; the UI hands over the pen and never lectures.
2. Progress feels like a playlist, not a report card. Gamified, earned, personal; never grades, gold stars, or teacher energy.
3. Jewish as invitation, not gate. Values-grounded entry points are always available and never required; no prior knowledge assumed, nothing heavy-handed.
4. Celebration is the destination. Warmth and anticipation build through the flow; the dashboard should feel like something is coming.
5. Parents get confidence, not control. The parent surface is calm, legible, and reassuring; it reports the journey without steering it.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA. Body text at 4.5:1 or better, large text at 3:1. Full keyboard operability for quiz and dashboards. Reduced-motion alternatives for every animation. Copy assumes zero Jewish literacy; any Jewish concept is glossed in plain words. Diverse, modern, interfaith families reflected in all sample content.
