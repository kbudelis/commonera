# REFS — KETUBBAH (genre precedent for the pledge card)

Research lane deliverable · July 10, 2026. The ketubbah — the decorated Jewish
marriage covenant — is the closest historical genre to our pledge card: a
personal covenant document, made beautiful so it will be displayed and kept.
Catalog references only; no assets downloaded. Licenses verified per item and
noted honestly (Manchester images are CC BY-NC — reference use only; Commons
items listed are PD reproductions of PD works).

## 2 · CONSTRUCTION RULES

Measured from the ledger items above. BINDING = consistent across all three
regional traditions (Italian, Sephardic, Persian); SUGGESTIVE = strong in one
tradition, optional borrowing.

**R1 — Portrait, always. [BINDING]**
Every displayed ketubbah is a portrait sheet. Measured ratios: Persian 1:1.32
(ref 6), Ancona 1:1.50 (ref 9), Rome 1:1.62 (ref 8). Range: 1:1.3–1:1.62.
Our card's 1200 × 1600 (1:1.33) sits at the Persian end of the attested range.

**R2 — The frame is a zone, not a line. [BINDING]**
The ornamental border is a major compositional mass. Across refs 1, 6, 8, 9
the combined border consumes roughly 30–45% of sheet width (i.e., 15–22% per
side), leaving a text field of ~55–70% of sheet width. A hairline rule is not
a ketubbah frame; a band you could rest a finger on is.

**R3 — Frames nest, at least two deep. [BINDING]**
Minimum two concentric frames with breathing parchment between them: an outer
ornamental frame and an inner rule or verse band immediately guarding the
text (refs 6, 8: verses form *both* inner and outer frames; ref 8 doubles the
micrography). The text field is the innermost, quietest zone.

**R4 — The largest letters are the opening words, top of the text field, centered. [BINDING]**
Hierarchy is carried by letter size (ref 12: Rome's whole school is
calligraphic). The enlarged opening formula sits at the head of the text
field, on the axis, directly under the frame's apex. Body text is small,
even, unornamented ink.

**R5 — Names are honored inside the text, by size and gold — never exiled to a caption. [BINDING]**
Bride's and groom's names are written in enlarged square letters (often gilt)
*within* the covenant text (refs 8, 9, 12). The person belongs inside the
sentence of the covenant, set larger, not appended below it.

**R6 — The text stands in a gate. [SUGGESTIVE — Italian/Sephardic; Persian uses rectangular panels]**
The canonical Italian framing is an arch on columns (ref 9), read explicitly
as a portal: Psalm 118:20, "this is the gate of the LORD; the righteous shall
enter," lettered in gold at the apex. Entering the document is arriving
somewhere. Sephardic-Ottoman tops use peaked/house shapes (ref 4, UW Stroum);
Persian keeps rectangular carpet panels (refs 6, 7).

**R7 — Bilateral symmetry about the vertical axis. [BINDING]**
The structural frame is symmetric left-right in every tradition (ref 4:
"almost exactly symmetrical"). Asymmetry lives only in small organic detail
(a flower's turn, a signature). Note: our three-stars motif is an asymmetric
cluster by token contract — it plays the role of the small sanctioned
asymmetry and must stay small.

**R8 — Micrography: the frame is built of meaningful words. [SUGGESTIVE — the Roman/Ancona calligraphic school's signature]**
Minor sacred texts (Book of Ruth, Song of Songs, Proverbs) written in
minuscule square letters form the physical geometry of the border (ref 8);
lines of micrography may crisscross into diamond lattices. The text chosen is
always *about* the covenant (Ruth = a marriage story placed above a
marriage). The border text runs continuous and unbroken — it is structure,
not caption.

**R9 — Color placement is role-based. [BINDING]**
Gold: opening words, names, columns, crowns, apex inscriptions — whatever
carries honor or light (refs 5, 9). Red: grounds and fields *behind*
ornament, never the covenant text itself (ref 9: red fields with
spared-ground florals). Blue: spandrels and sky zones between arch and frame
(ref 9). The contract body: plain dark ink on undyed parchment, always.

**R10 — The foot is quiet and administrative. [BINDING]**
The bottom zone holds the small matter — dowry sums, witness signatures, and
in Persia sometimes the illuminator's own signature in the bottom margin.
Smallest text on the sheet; no ornament competes there.

**R11 — The top edge may be shaped. [SUGGESTIVE — Italian]**
Parchment shoulders produced curved, wavy top edges that the design embraces
(ref 9); many sheets are die-cut or scalloped at top. The document announces
itself as an object, not a rectangle of paper.

## 3 · Application to the pledge card

Current state (src/artifact/Pledge.tsx): 1200 × 1600 viewBox, single 0.9px
vermilion rule at inset 92, one pass of 7px micrography on that rectangle,
three stars at top, 868px-wide word-panel, name/timing lines, foot motto.
The bones are right; by R2/R3 the frame is currently ~40× too light.

**Proportions.** Keep 1200 × 1600 (1:1.33) — attested at the Persian end of
the range (R1). No change needed; if the card ever grows a "display" variant,
1:1.5 (1200 × 1800) moves it toward the Italian golden age.

**Frame (the big move).** Promote the border from hairline to zone (R2, R3):
a border band of 96–144px per side (8–12% of width — the modest end of the
historical 15–22%, appropriate to mobile legibility). Structure it as two
concentric vermilion hairline rules (e.g., insets 64 and 176) with the
micrography running between them — the user's own pledge words *are* the
band's fill (R8, and Law 2: the word is the ornament). Upgrades within
contract: run the micrography path 2–3 concentric passes, or let two passes
crisscross into the Roman diamond lattice at the corners. Keep it ink at
~0.78 opacity — micrography is structure in ink, never gold.

**Word-panel as gate.** The intention panel already sits centered, top-third,
in a double gold-ember rule frame — correct per R4. Borrow the portal (R6):
give the panel's top edge a shallow arch (a single quadratic curve on the
outer rule, rising ~40–60px at the axis) springing from straight sides —
abstract pilasters, no capitals, no figuration. The three gold stars sit
above the arch apex exactly where Ancona lettered its gate verse in gold:
the stars are our apex inscription. Panel width stays ~72% of card width
(868/1200) — inside the historical text-field range.

**Name.** Per R5, the name is currently honored correctly *inside* a sentence
("[Name] is keeping a Digital Shabbat") — keep that. Raise its rank: EB
Garamond 600 at the current 48px is right, but the name itself may carry the
gold-ember color (not full gold — gold stays reserved for the intention and
stars, R9).

**Timing summary and summary line.** Body register: small, even, plain ink
(R4). Current 28px Inter is correct. Do not ornament.

**Foot.** "One day, set apart." above the bottom border band, smallest
display text on the card, with the existing thin gold-ember rule — this is
the quiet administrative foot (R10). Correct as built.

**Color audit against R9.** Gold → intention words, three stars only.
Gold-ember → rules, arch, name accent. Vermilion → the two frame hairlines
and nothing else (token contract already says "rare accent only" — the
historical role matches: red frames, never red text). Ink → everything the
user must read. Parchment stays undyed; no large fills.

**Do NOT borrow.** Putti, cherubs, and allegorical personifications (Rome,
refs 8–10 era); zodiac medallions and tribe vignettes (refs 1, 10); Temple/
Jerusalem cityscapes (ref 10, NLI); the Sacrifice of Isaac and any biblical
figures (ref 9's cartouche); Qajar lion-and-sun, animals, hunting scenes
(refs 6, 13); family heraldic emblems (ref 1). Our kernel is aniconic (Law
4): we take the ketubbah's *geometry* — nested frames, the gate, symmetry,
word-built ornament, role-based gold — and leave every figure at the museum.
